I have owned both. The Comandante C40 has been on my counter since 2020. The 1Zpresso JX-Pro joined in late 2022, when I needed a second grinder for travel and could not justify a second Comandante at full price. What was supposed to be a temporary pairing turned into three years of side-by-side use, and I have a clearer answer now than I did when I bought either one.

The short version: the JX-Pro is the better value. The C40 is the better object. Whether the difference matters depends on what you want a grinder to be.

## Two companies with very different DNA

**Comandante** is a small German manufacturer based in Solingen, the historic blade-making city. Their entire product line revolves around one product — the C40 — and they have iterated it carefully across roughly 30 batches since 2014. The company is run with the slow, deliberate cadence you associate with German hand tools.

**1Zpresso** is a Taiwanese manufacturer founded in 2017. They moved fast, released grinders aggressively (J, JX, JX-Pro, K-Plus, K-Max, ZP6, X-Ultra, and on and on), and built a reputation for high precision at prices below the European competition. The JX-Pro is their filter-focused grinder in the mid-tier, sitting below the K-series.

These backgrounds matter because they show up in the products. The Comandante feels like one product made for a long time. The JX-Pro feels like the result of rapid iteration in a competitive market.

## Burr design: the heart of any grinder

The Comandante uses a 39mm conical burr made from what they call "Nitro Blade" — a high-nitrogen martensitic stainless steel hardened in a proprietary process. The geometry is conventional but the material and tolerance are what Comandante is famous for. The burr is sharp, holds its edge for years of daily use, and produces a particle distribution that prioritizes uniformity at filter grind sizes.

The 1Zpresso JX-Pro uses a 48mm conical burr with a heptagonal (seven-sided) cutting profile. The larger diameter means more burr surface engaging the bean per turn, which translates into faster grinding. The heptagonal geometry is 1Zpresso's design choice — more cutting edges per revolution, intended to produce a cleaner cut and reduce fines.

In practical particle distribution, the two are close enough that I cannot reliably tell them apart in a blind cup at filter grind sizes. The Comandante might produce slightly fewer fines at the very fine end of filter (20 clicks and below). The JX-Pro might be slightly more uniform at the coarse end (4+ rotations). Both produce competition-acceptable cups. Anyone who tells you one is dramatically more uniform than the other in cup is either selling something or hearing what they expect.

For more on what particle distribution actually does to your cup, see [Coffee Grind Size: The Complete Guide](/articles/coffee-grind-size-guide).

## Build quality: this is where the gap opens

The Comandante body is a CNC-machined aluminum tube wrapped in a wood sleeve (walnut, oak, maple, or one of the seasonal limited editions). The crank handle is solid wood. The catch jar comes in glass with a rubber sleeve. The whole grinder weighs about 670g and feels substantial without being heavy. Every interface — the lid, the catch jar threads, the handle attachment — has the precision of something machined to within a fraction of a millimeter. After five years of daily use, mine looks identical to the day I bought it.

The 1Zpresso JX-Pro body is anodized aluminum throughout. The crank handle is metal with a wooden knob. The catch jar is aluminum. It weighs about 720g — slightly heavier than the Comandante. The build is excellent for the price. The threads are clean. The bearings are smooth. But it does not feel like a piece of generational equipment the way the Comandante does. It feels like a very well-made tool. Which is what it is.

The difference matters less than I thought it would when I unboxed the JX-Pro, and more than I expected after three years. The Comandante still gives me a small dose of pleasure every morning. The JX-Pro is just my grinder. Both grind coffee. One is also a satisfying object.

## Grind setting precision: clicks per revolution

The Comandante uses a stepless adjustment system that is described as stepped — there are detents (clicks) but they are extremely fine, and many users adjust between clicks for espresso. The C40 has approximately 30 clicks per full rotation of the adjustment ring, and you typically use 18-30 clicks for filter brewing.

The 1Zpresso JX-Pro uses a click-step system with 40 clicks per rotation, which 1Zpresso calls "external adjustment" — you turn a numbered dial on the outside of the grinder rather than adjusting from inside. The numbered dial is a quality-of-life upgrade that Comandante does not match. You can read your setting at a glance, return to a previous setting precisely, and dial in a new bean by writing down "1.8 turns + 4 clicks" and being able to come back to exactly that.

For someone who brews multiple coffees with different grind settings — different beans for V60 vs Aeropress, for example — the JX-Pro's external numbered dial is genuinely better. The Comandante's stepless feel is more elegant but harder to communicate or return to.

## Best brew methods

The Comandante is optimized for filter brewing. It can grind for espresso, but the click resolution at the fine end is limited and it is genuinely a stretch. Comandante owners who want to do espresso often buy a Red Clix axle (sold separately) which doubles the click resolution at the fine end. Without it, espresso dial-in is frustrating.

The JX-Pro is also a filter-first grinder, but its 40-clicks-per-rotation system gives it more usable resolution at the espresso end out of the box. It can do single-cup espresso adequately. For serious espresso work, 1Zpresso's K-series or J-Max are designed specifically for that purpose and are better choices than the JX-Pro.

If you only brew filter — V60, Kalita, Chemex, Aeropress — both are excellent. If you brew filter and dabble in espresso, the JX-Pro is more flexible without accessories. If you only do espresso, neither of these is the right tool. See [Espresso at Home: The Complete Guide](/articles/espresso-at-home-guide) for espresso-specific gear.

## Price: where the value calculation lands

The Comandante C40 retails around $300 USD. The 1Zpresso JX-Pro retails around $180 USD. That is a $120 gap, which is not nothing — it is roughly the cost of a quality pour-over setup, or 20 bags of specialty coffee.

If you are buying your first hand grinder and you are not sure how deep into the hobby you want to go, the JX-Pro is the more rational purchase. You get 90% of the Comandante's grind quality for 60% of the price, and the external click adjustment is arguably more practical.

If you have been brewing for a while, you know you will keep at it for years, and you want a single piece of equipment that you will not feel the need to upgrade later, the Comandante is worth the premium. The wood-and-aluminum build, the slow-iteration design, and the resale value — used Comandantes hold price remarkably well — combine to make it less expensive than the sticker suggests over a long enough timeline.

## Step calibration system

The JX-Pro arrives with a clearly numbered scale and a documented "zero point" — you grind down until the burrs touch, mark that as zero, then dial out to your brewing setting. The system is well-documented and beginner-friendly.

The Comandante does not have an external scale. Owners count clicks from a chosen reference (often "fully closed plus N clicks") and write it down. Some owners label their grinder with stickers. This is a solved problem in the JX-Pro and an unsolved one in the Comandante. For people who switch beans frequently, this is a real friction point on the C40.

## Ergonomics over long sessions

I usually grind 18g for a single V60. The Comandante takes about 60-70 seconds. The JX-Pro takes about 45-55 seconds — the larger 48mm burr genuinely chews through beans faster. Over a week of single cups this is not a meaningful difference. If you are brewing for two or three people every morning, the JX-Pro's speed advantage compounds.

The crank length on both is similar but the Comandante's handle has a slightly more comfortable grip shape for me. The JX-Pro's metal-and-wood handle is fine but a touch less ergonomic over long grinds. Neither will give you a hand cramp at filter doses. Both will if you try to grind 40g of espresso-fine coffee.

## Retention

Both grinders are low-retention by design. In my testing, both leave less than 0.3g of coffee inside the grinder between brews when used at filter grind sizes. The JX-Pro has a slight edge here because of its wider exit chute. Neither retention figure is high enough to affect cup quality unless you are switching between very different coffees back-to-back, in which case a single-dose blowout with a few grams of the new coffee handles it.

## FAQ

**1. Is the Comandante C40 worth twice the price of the JX-Pro?**
For most people, no. For people who value the build quality and want a grinder for the next decade, yes. The grind quality difference in the cup is small.

**2. Which is better for V60?**
A tie at the cup level. Both produce excellent V60 brews at 18-22 clicks (Comandante) or 1.8-2.2 turns (JX-Pro). For V60-specific guidance see [The Hario V60 Complete Guide](/articles/hario-v60-vs-kalita-wave) — wait, the relevant guide is on V60 brewing technique under [How to Brew the Perfect Pour Over](/articles/how-to-brew-perfect-pour-over).

**3. Can I use either for espresso?**
The JX-Pro can, with reasonable results for single shots. The Comandante can with a Red Clix axle accessory ($60+) but it is awkward. Neither is a serious espresso grinder.

**4. Which is faster?**
The JX-Pro, by about 15-20%, due to the larger 48mm burr.

**5. Do they need to be cleaned?**
Both should be brushed out every 4-6 weeks and fully disassembled and cleaned every 6 months. Both come apart easily with no special tools. The JX-Pro is slightly easier to fully disassemble.

**6. Can I take either traveling?**
Yes. Both are similar in size. The JX-Pro packs slightly more compactly because the handle detaches more easily. The Comandante is more visually conspicuous in luggage if you care about that.

**7. Will the burrs wear out?**
Not in any meaningful timeframe for home use. Both manufacturers rate their burrs for hundreds of kilograms of coffee, which is decades of typical home brewing.

**8. Should I buy electric instead?**
If grinding 18g once a day is a chore for you, yes. See [Manual vs Electric Coffee Grinders](/articles/manual-vs-electric-coffee-grinders) for the full discussion.

## Honest downsides

**Comandante C40:**
- Expensive at $300, especially given non-premium competitors that come close on cup quality.
- No external numbered dial for setting recall.
- Espresso requires the Red Clix accessory and even then is a stretch.
- Wood handle can crack if you over-tighten it.
- Wait times during back-orders can be long.

**1Zpresso JX-Pro:**
- Build quality, while excellent for the price, does not have the heirloom feel of the Comandante.
- Anodized aluminum can scratch and looks worn faster.
- No premium aesthetic — it looks like a tool, not an object.
- Resale value is decent but not exceptional.
- The brand releases new models constantly, which can make any specific model feel less special over time.

## My current take

I own both. I use the Comandante on weekend mornings when I am not in a hurry and want the ritual to feel like a small ceremony. I use the JX-Pro on weekday mornings when I want my coffee fast and I have already decided which bean and grind setting I am using. Neither has produced a noticeably different cup over three years of A/B testing.

If I had to keep only one, it would be the Comandante — for reasons that have nothing to do with cup quality and everything to do with how it feels in my hand. If I were starting over today, knowing what I know, I would buy the JX-Pro and put the $120 saved toward a better gooseneck kettle and another bag of coffee.

Both are great. The C40 is the romantic answer. The JX-Pro is the rational one. Pick the one that matches who you are when you are buying coffee gear, not the one that matches who you wish you were.
