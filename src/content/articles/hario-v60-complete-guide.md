I have brewed coffee in a Hario V60 almost every morning for nine years. I have owned the plastic, the ceramic, the glass, and — for the past three years — the copper version. I have used it badly, I have used it well, and on a handful of mornings I have used it so well that the cup tasted like it belonged in a competition lineup. This guide is everything I have learned, condensed into one place.

If you want one dripper that can win World Brewers Cup titles (it has, repeatedly), forgive a beginner, and grow with you over a decade, the V60 is it. But it is not the easiest dripper on the market. It rewards attention and punishes laziness. Let's get you on the rewarded side.

## The design philosophy: why a 60-degree cone matters

The "60" in V60 refers to the angle of the cone — 60 degrees, measured from the centerline. Hario, a Japanese glassware company that has been making lab equipment since 1921, released this dripper in 2004. Three design choices define it:

**The 60-degree cone.** A steeper cone than the Kalita Wave or Melitta forces the slurry of coffee and water to flow toward a single point at the bottom. This creates a longer contact time as water travels through the bed, and it allows the brewer to control flow rate by pour pattern alone.

**The spiral ribs.** Look inside any V60 and you will see ribs that spiral up the cone rather than running straight. The spiral keeps the paper filter from collapsing flat against the cone wall, leaving an air channel for CO2 to escape and for water to flow. The spiral is also why V60 filters cannot be substituted with random conical filters from another brand — the geometry will not match.

**The single large hole.** Unlike the Kalita Wave (three small holes) or the Melitta (one tiny hole), the V60 has a single large hole at the bottom. This means flow rate is controlled almost entirely by your grind size and pour technique, not by the dripper geometry. That is the V60's superpower and its trap. With skill, you control everything. Without skill, every mistake compounds.

## V60 model variants: which one is right for you

Hario sells the V60 in five materials. Each behaves slightly differently because thermal mass changes how heat is retained during the brew.

### Plastic (AS resin)

The cheapest option, around $8. Lightweight, indestructible, and — counterintuitively — one of the best performers because plastic has very low thermal conductivity. It does not steal heat from your brew. Champion Tetsu Kasuya famously prefers plastic for this reason. The downside: it looks cheap, and over years of use the plastic can pick up coffee oils and discolor.

**Best for:** travel, the office, beginners who do not want to invest, and people who genuinely do not care about aesthetics.

### Ceramic

Around $25. The most popular choice. Ceramic looks beautiful, feels substantial, and once preheated holds temperature very well. The catch is right there: you must preheat it. Pouring 92°C water into a cold ceramic dripper drops your brew temperature by 4-6°C in the first few seconds, which matters more than people admit.

**Best for:** home use, daily ritual, anyone who appreciates the weight of a ceramic object in their morning routine.

### Glass

Around $30. Clear, elegant, lets you see the bloom. Thermally similar to ceramic but more fragile. I have broken two over the years.

**Best for:** people who like the visual theater of brewing and have a stable countertop.

### Copper

Around $80-100. The most thermally conductive material in the lineup. Copper heats fast and transfers heat fast — it stabilizes brew temperature better than any other version once preheated. It also looks stunning and ages with a natural patina. The downside: it is expensive, the lining can scratch, and copper is heavier than people expect.

**Best for:** enthusiasts who brew daily and want the best thermal performance, and people who like equipment that gets more beautiful with age.

### Metal (stainless steel)

Around $50. The forgotten sibling. Decent thermal performance, basically indestructible, but the visual is utilitarian. I find it fine but uninspiring.

**Best for:** travel, outdoor use, people who want something nearly unbreakable.

If you are buying your first V60, get the plastic and spend the savings on a better grinder. If you already have a good grinder, get ceramic or copper depending on your budget.

## V60-01 vs V60-02: size matters more than you think

Hario sells the V60 in three sizes — 01, 02, and 03 — referring to the brew capacity. The 01 brews 1-2 cups (up to about 250ml). The 02 brews 1-4 cups (up to about 500ml). The 03 is for serving large groups and you almost certainly do not need it.

Here is the part nobody tells beginners: the 02 is not just a bigger 01. The geometry is similar but the larger cone means a deeper coffee bed at the same dose. A 15g dose in the 02 sits in a thinner layer than the same 15g in the 01, which changes extraction. If you brew single cups (12-18g) most of the time, get the 01. If you brew for two people regularly (25-35g), get the 02. Buy the size you actually use, not the size you imagine you might use someday.

## The 4:6 method (Tetsu Kasuya)

In 2016, Tetsu Kasuya won the World Brewers Cup using a method so simple it sounded like a joke. He divided the total water into 40% and 60%, then split each portion further. The 40% controls flavor balance (acidity vs sweetness). The 60% controls strength.

For a 20g dose at a 1:15 ratio (300g of water), the recipe is:

1. **Bloom (0:00 to 0:45):** Pour 60g of water (the first 20% of the 40%). Wait.
2. **Pour 2 (0:45 to 1:30):** Pour to 120g total (40% complete). Smaller pour for sweetness, larger for acidity.
3. **Pour 3 (1:30 to 2:15):** Pour to 180g total.
4. **Pour 4 (2:15 to 3:00):** Pour to 240g total.
5. **Pour 5 (3:00 to 3:45):** Pour to 300g total.
6. **Drawdown:** Total time 3:30-4:00.

The genius of the 4:6 method is that you get two independent dials. If your coffee tastes sour, make the first 40% one big pour for more sweetness. If it tastes flat, make it two small pours for brighter acidity. If it tastes weak, do the 60% in two pours instead of three. If it tastes too strong, do it in four pours.

## Pulse-pour technique (and why Lance Hedrick won't let you off easy)

The 4:6 method is an example of pulse pouring — multiple discrete pours rather than one continuous stream. The other school is the single continuous pour, championed by brewers like Lance Hedrick.

Hedrick's signature recipe uses a much higher ratio (1:18 or 1:20), one continuous spiral pour from the bloom to the end, and an aggressive WDT-style stir during the bloom. The result is a higher-extraction, tea-like cup that tastes lighter and more transparent. It demands a steady kettle hand and a consistent grind.

Both approaches work. Pulse pouring is more forgiving for inconsistent kettles. Continuous pouring is more forgiving for thinking — once you start, you do not have to count anymore.

## Grind size: medium-fine, around 600 microns

For V60, aim for a medium-fine grind — finer than drip machine grind, coarser than espresso. In micron terms, around 600µm at the median particle size. In Comandante click terms, 18-22 clicks. In 1Zpresso JX-Pro terms, 1.8-2.4 turns.

If your brew finishes too fast (under 2:30), grind finer. If it stalls (over 4:30), grind coarser. If it tastes sour and weak, grind finer. If it tastes bitter and astringent, grind coarser. The grind is the largest single variable you control after the coffee itself. A complete breakdown lives in [Coffee Grind Size: The Complete Guide](/articles/coffee-grind-size-guide).

## Water temperature: 90 to 94°C

V60 likes hot water. For light roasts, push to 93-94°C to extract enough sweetness. For medium roasts, 91-92°C is comfortable. For dark roasts, drop to 88-90°C to avoid pulling out bitterness. The exact reasoning is in [Water Temperature for Coffee Brewing: The Definitive Guide](/articles/water-temperature-guide).

A common mistake: people pour boiling water directly without checking. By the time it hits the bed of a cold ceramic dripper, you might be brewing at 84°C. Preheat the dripper, the server, and the cup with hot water before you start. This single change improved my brews more than any new piece of equipment ever did.

## Filter rinse: not optional

Hario's V60 paper filters have a noticeable papery taste if you skip the rinse. Place the filter in the dripper, pour hot water around it until it is fully saturated, let it drain, and discard the water. This serves three purposes: removes paper taste, seals the filter to the dripper ribs, and preheats the dripper and server in one step.

I use bleached filters (the white ones). They are cleaner-tasting than unbleached natural filters, and the bleaching process used by Hario is oxygen-based, not chlorine. There is no health concern. Skip the unbleached unless you specifically prefer their slightly fuller body.

## Server choice: get the matching one

The Hario V60 Range Server (the glass carafe with measurement marks) is purpose-designed to hold the dripper perfectly upright and to chill rapidly so you can refrigerate leftovers. The 600ml size pairs with both the 01 and 02 dripper. Generic carafes work but the V60 cone often sits crooked on them, which causes uneven extraction.

## Scale recommendation

A scale is non-negotiable for V60. Pour rate, total weight, and timing are all things you cannot eyeball within useful tolerance. ## The three most common mistakes

**1. Pouring too aggressively in the center.** A heavy center pour drills a hole through the bed and water channels straight down without extracting evenly. Pour gently and spiral outward.

**2. Skipping the bloom.** The bloom (first 30-45 seconds with just enough water to wet the grounds) lets CO2 escape so the rest of the brew can extract evenly. Skipping it on fresh coffee guarantees an uneven cup.

**3. Wrong water.** Distilled water tastes flat. Tap water with high mineral content tastes muddled. Use filtered water with around 50-150 ppm total hardness. The full breakdown is in [The Water Recipe Guide for Coffee](/articles/water-recipe-guide).

## FAQ

**1. Can I use a regular paper filter in a V60?**
No. The 60-degree cone with spiral ribs requires V60-specific filters. Misfit filters either collapse against the wall (no flow) or leave gaps (channeling).

**2. Is the V60 better than the Kalita Wave?**
Different, not better. V60 is more flexible in both directions — clearer at its best and worse at its worst. Kalita Wave is more forgiving and consistent. See [Hario V60 vs Kalita Wave](/articles/hario-v60-vs-kalita-wave).

**3. How long should the brew take?**
For a 1:15 ratio with 15-20g of coffee, total brew time should be 3:00-4:00 from first pour to drawdown. Outside this range, adjust grind.

**4. Why does my V60 brew taste sour?**
Underextraction. Try a finer grind, hotter water, longer brew time, or a higher ratio (1:16 instead of 1:15).

**5. Why does my V60 brew taste bitter?**
Overextraction or scorching. Try a coarser grind, cooler water, shorter brew time, or a lower ratio (1:14).

**6. Do I need a gooseneck kettle?**
Yes, basically. The V60 is a precision dripper and pour control matters. A regular kettle pours too aggressively to control. Even a cheap gooseneck makes a noticeable difference.

**7. Can I brew espresso with a V60?**
No. The V60 is a gravity dripper. Espresso requires 9 bars of pressure.

**8. Plastic vs ceramic — does it really matter?**
At the margin, yes. Ceramic looks better and feels nicer but requires preheating. Plastic is thermally inert and travels better. Many champions use plastic. Pick by use case.

## Honest downsides

The V60 is technique-sensitive. A bad pour will produce a bad cup, full stop. The single hole means channeling is a real risk — water finds the path of least resistance through the bed and bypasses parts of the coffee. The spiral ribs collect coffee residue over time and need scrubbing. The plastic version looks cheap. The copper version is expensive.

If you want a dripper that absorbs your mistakes, look at the Kalita Wave or Origami. If you want a dripper that lets you express yourself — and that occasionally produces a cup so good you will think about it for the rest of the day — the V60 is the one.
