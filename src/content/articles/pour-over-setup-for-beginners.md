I have spent more money on coffee gear than I would like to admit, and I have learned almost everything I know the hard way. The first pour over kit I bought was a beautiful matching set in a glossy box, and within six months I had replaced every single piece in it. So when friends ask me how to start brewing pour over at home without setting their wallet on fire, I now answer with three specific builds at three specific price points. This article is that answer, written down, with every component justified.

Pour over is the friendliest entry point into specialty coffee for a few simple reasons. The gear is cheap compared to espresso, the technique forgives you while you learn, it makes a single great cup faster than a French press, and the brewing process itself becomes a small daily ritual that a lot of people genuinely look forward to. Unlike a $1,500 espresso machine that demands a one-time decision, a pour over setup can be assembled piece by piece, upgraded in any order, and lived with for years.

Before I show you the three builds, here is what you actually need and why.

## What a Complete Pour Over Setup Includes

A real pour over setup is six items, not one. Skipping any of them is the reason most beginner pour overs taste sour, weak, or bitter.

**A dripper.** This is the cone or wedge that holds the coffee bed. The Hario V60, the Kalita Wave, and the Origami are the three shapes that matter. Each one brews differently and pairs with a different style of pouring.

**Filters.** Bleached or unbleached paper filters specific to your dripper shape. Generic filters do not work — a V60 filter and a Kalita Wave filter are completely different objects.

**A grinder.** This is the single most important piece of gear in the whole kit. I will say it bluntly: a $100 grinder paired with a $20 dripper makes better coffee than a $300 dripper paired with a blade grinder. Grind quality controls extraction more than any other variable.

**A scale.** Pour over is a recipe. You need to know that you used 18 grams of coffee and 300 grams of water, and you need to know how long the brew took. A scale with 0.1 gram resolution and a built-in timer is the move.

**A gooseneck kettle.** A normal kitchen kettle pours water in an uncontrollable cannonball. A gooseneck kettle gives you a pencil-thin stream you can direct exactly where you want it. Stovetop is fine to start; a variable-temperature electric kettle is a real upgrade later.

**A server or carafe.** The vessel the coffee drips into. Many drippers ship with a matching server, but a clear glass carafe lets you see the brew and is more useful than people expect.

That is the full kit. Now let me explain the part most beginners get wrong.

## Why the Grinder Matters Most

If you take one thing from this article, take this: spend more on the grinder than on anything else. A pre-ground bag of coffee from a grocery store, no matter how nice the dripper, will produce a flat and stale cup. Coffee starts losing aroma within minutes of being ground. A blade grinder — the spinning propeller kind sold in supermarkets — produces wildly uneven particles, and uneven particles brew unevenly, which means the same cup will be over-extracted and under-extracted at the same time. That is the taste of bitter and sour together, and it is the reason a lot of people decide they "don't like pour over."

A real burr grinder produces a tight, consistent particle size. Even a $90 burr grinder will outperform any blade grinder ever made. This is why my $100 build leads with the grinder and works backward from there. If your budget is tight, buy the grinder first and pour into a paper towel taped to a mug if you have to. (Please don't actually do that. But the principle is real.)

I cover the entire grinder landscape under $100 in [my detailed grinder roundup](/articles/best-coffee-grinders-under-100), and the relationship between grind size and extraction is explained in [the grind size guide](/articles/coffee-grind-size-guide). If you want the deeper why, those are the rabbit holes.

## The $100 Build: Pure Function, Zero Vanity

This is the build I recommend to anyone testing the waters. It has no luxury parts, but every piece does its job, and the total experience is genuinely good. I drank coffee from a kit nearly identical to this for two years before I upgraded anything.

**Hario V60-01 ceramic dripper — about $25.** The V60 is the most-sold pour over dripper on earth for a reason. The 60-degree cone, the spiral ribs, and the large single hole at the bottom give you total control over flow rate. The 01 size brews one to two cups, which is the right size for a single drinker. Get the ceramic version — it holds heat better than the plastic, and only costs a few dollars more.

**Hario V60 paper filters, size 01, white — about $6 for 100.** Bleached filters do not taste like paper. Unbleached filters do, slightly, unless you rinse them thoroughly. I always recommend bleached for beginners.

**Timemore Chestnut C2 hand grinder — about $70.** This is the genuine sleeper of the entire pour over world. Stainless steel burrs, a smooth crank, consistent grind, and a price that should not be possible. It grinds enough coffee for a single cup in about 45 seconds. Yes, your arm will get a little tired. No, you will not regret it.

**A cheap kitchen scale — about $15.** Any digital scale with 1 gram resolution and a tare function will work to start. Look for the small flat ones sold for jewelry or food prep on Amazon. Get a phone timer going next to it and you have a working brew rig.

**A stovetop gooseneck kettle — about $30.** Bonavita and Coffee Gator both make basic stovetop goosenecks under $30. You will not have temperature control — you will boil water and let it sit for thirty seconds before pouring. That works fine for most coffees.

**Server: skip it.** Brew straight into your mug. The V60-01 sits perfectly on top of a standard 12-ounce mug.

**Total: roughly $146 if you buy everything new, closer to $100 if you find the kettle on sale or already own a scale.** I have included a small overrun because nobody actually pays sticker on all six items in the same week.

What this build skips: temperature control, weight precision below one gram, a built-in timer, an aesthetic kitchen presence. None of those things change the taste of the coffee. You will brew a cup that is 90% as good as my $300 setup with this kit, as long as you follow [a real recipe](/articles/how-to-brew-perfect-pour-over).

## The $200 Build: The Sweet Spot

This is the build I would actually buy if I were starting over today. It is the point where each upgrade gives you something concrete, and beyond it the returns start to taper. If you can stretch the budget once, stretch it to here.

**Hario V60-02 or Kalita Wave 185 — about $35.** Step up to the larger V60-02 if you brew for two people sometimes, or switch to the Kalita Wave 185 for an easier, more forgiving brew. The Wave's flat bottom and three small holes slow the flow and even out small pouring mistakes. I drink both. If you want help deciding, I wrote [a full V60 vs Wave comparison](/articles/hario-v60-vs-kalita-wave) — read that first.

**Filters — about $8.** Same deal as before, in the right shape for your chosen dripper.

**Baratza Encore electric grinder — about $170.** This is the single biggest jump in this build. The Encore is the entry point into electric grinding for pour over. Conical steel burrs, 40 grind settings, push-button operation, and parts you can replace for the next decade. I have owned mine for six years and it still grinds like the day I bought it. It is loud and it is plain, but it just works.

**Hario V60 drip scale — about $50.** A real coffee scale with a built-in timer, 0.1 gram resolution, and a flat profile that fits the V60 perfectly. The timer auto-starts when you tare; the display is bright. I cover the whole scale category in [my scale guide](/articles/best-coffee-scales).

**Fellow Stagg pour over kettle (non-EKG, stovetop) — about $80.** The non-electric Stagg is a stovetop kettle, but with a flow-control spout that gives you essentially the same pouring experience as the electric version. You still boil water and wait, but the pour itself is on another level.

**Server: skip it again.** Or use a small Pyrex measuring cup if you want to see the brew.

**Total: roughly $343 at full retail, but realistically $250–280 with the grinder discounted (it is on sale somewhere most months).** Calling it the "$200 build" assumes you found at least one piece on sale. If your budget is hard-locked at $200, swap the Encore for a Timemore C3 ($90) and you can hit it.

What this build skips: temperature control, premium scale features, ultra-fast electric heating. The weak spot is still the kettle — boiling and waiting is fine, but it adds a minute to every brew. That is the upgrade I made first when I got to the next tier.

## The $300 Build: Where the Returns Get Subtle

This is the kit I currently use most mornings. Every piece is something I would buy again. The improvements over the $200 build are real but smaller than the jump from the $100 build to this one. If you are absolutely sure you love pour over, this is where you end up.

**Origami dripper or Kalita Wave 185 stainless steel — about $50.** The Origami is a Japanese dripper with twenty vertical ridges that you can use with either V60-style or Kalita Wave-style filters, which is a small superpower. The stainless Kalita Wave is the same dripper as before with the heat-retention bonus of metal. Either works. I use the Origami most days.

**Baratza Encore ESP — about $200.** The ESP version of the Encore adds finer grind settings useful for espresso, but the pour over performance is also improved over the original Encore — better burr alignment and a slightly larger range of useful settings. If you might ever try [manual espresso](/articles/best-manual-espresso-makers), this is the smarter buy.

**Acaia Lunar or Fellow Tally scale — $150–250.** The Lunar is the gold standard for coffee scales: 0.1 gram resolution, water-resistant, a beautiful UI, and an auto-mode that handles timing without any button pushing. The Tally is Fellow's newer, less expensive answer, and it is genuinely good. Either makes brewing feel like it is happening in slow motion. If your $300 budget is firm, the Tally fits; the Lunar pushes you over.

**Fellow Stagg EKG variable-temperature electric kettle — about $165.** This is, in my opinion, the best single upgrade in all of pour over. Set the temperature to 96 degrees Celsius, hit start, the kettle reaches that exact temperature in about three minutes and holds it for thirty. No boiling, no waiting, no thermometer. The pouring spout is the same as the stovetop Stagg. I have bought this kettle three times — once for me, twice as gifts. Everyone keeps it. I cover the whole category in [the gooseneck kettle guide](/articles/best-gooseneck-kettles).

**Filters and a glass server — about $30.** Treat yourself to the matching server now if you want one.

**Total: roughly $600 at full retail.** This is a "$300 build" only if you stagger the purchases and catch sales. Realistically this is the kit you build to over a year, not in a single weekend.

What this build skips: nothing meaningful for pour over. The next upgrades are different drippers for variety, a temperature-controlled water source, or moving sideways into espresso.

## Upgrade Path

If you are starting at the $100 build, this is the order I would replace pieces:

1. **Kettle first.** Going from stovetop to a Stagg EKG is the single most-felt change.
2. **Grinder second.** Manual grinding gets old after a year. Moving to an Encore is the second-biggest quality-of-life jump.
3. **Scale third.** A real coffee scale with auto-timing changes the rhythm of brewing in a way you do not realize until you have it.
4. **Dripper last.** The dripper is the cheapest piece and the one beginners obsess over. It matters least.

## The Honest Downsides

Pour over is not perfect. A few things to know before you commit.

It is slow. A single cup takes three to four minutes of active brewing, not counting heating water and grinding. If you need coffee in ninety seconds at 6 a.m., a French press or moka pot is faster.

It rewards practice. Your first ten cups will be inconsistent. Your fiftieth cup will be good. This is fine — most people enjoy the learning — but if you hate variance, an automatic drip machine like the Moccamaster or [Fellow Aiden](/articles/fellow-aiden-vs-moccamaster) is a better fit.

It is single-cup. Brewing for four people at once with a V60 is annoying. The Kalita Wave 185 helps a little, but for groups, switch to a Chemex or an automatic.

It is sensitive to water. If your tap water is heavy in minerals, your coffee will taste muddy no matter how good the gear is. A simple filter pitcher fixes this for most homes. I cover [water temperature](/articles/water-temperature-guide) in detail elsewhere; water composition is the next rabbit hole.

## FAQ

**Do I really need a gooseneck kettle?**
For pour over, yes. The point of pour over is to control where, when, and how fast the water hits the coffee bed. A normal kettle dumps water unevenly and you will get channeling — water punching a hole through one part of the bed and ignoring the rest. Even a $30 stovetop gooseneck solves this completely.

**Can I use pre-ground coffee?**
You can, but you should not. Pre-ground coffee starts losing flavor within fifteen minutes of grinding. By the time it gets to your kitchen from the bag in the store, ninety percent of what makes the coffee interesting is gone. Buy whole beans and grind right before brewing. This single change matters more than any equipment upgrade.

**Is the Hario V60 really the best dripper for beginners?**
It is the most flexible dripper, but not the most forgiving. The Kalita Wave is genuinely easier to brew well with, because the flat bottom and small holes hide minor pouring mistakes. If you want to start on easy mode, start with a Wave and switch to a V60 after a month if you want more control.

**How much coffee should I buy at once?**
Buy in 250-gram or 340-gram bags and finish them within three to four weeks of the roast date. Coffee tastes best between three and twenty days off roast for most filter coffees. Buying a large bulk bag and watching it stale out over two months is a common beginner mistake.

**Does a $30 scale work as well as a $200 one?**
For grams of coffee and grams of water, yes. The expensive scales add water resistance, faster response time, an auto-timer that starts when you pour, and a much nicer screen. None of those change the cup. They change the brewing experience, which matters more than people expect once you have brewed for a year.

**Should I get plastic, ceramic, or glass dripper material?**
Ceramic holds heat best and feels nice; preheat it with a rinse before brewing. Plastic is light, cheap, and travels well. Glass looks great but is fragile. Metal (the Origami's optional copper, the Wave's stainless) holds heat very well but is the most expensive. Functionally they all brew similar coffee — pick what you like looking at.

**Can I use the same setup for cold brew or other methods?**
The grinder, scale, and kettle (for the heated step of some methods) all carry over. The dripper does not. If you want to make [cold brew or iced coffee](/articles/cold-brew-vs-iced-coffee), or move into AeroPress, those are separate small purchases — but the core kit transfers.

**What is the absolute cheapest acceptable starting setup?**
A Timemore C2 grinder, a plastic V60-02 ($10), filters ($6), a $15 kitchen scale, and any kettle with a gooseneck spout ($25 stovetop). About $130. I would not recommend going lower than this. Below that price you start hitting blade grinders and bad kettles, and the cup quality falls off a cliff.

## Final Thoughts

The right pour over setup is the one you will actually use every morning. A $600 kit that intimidates you is worse than a $100 kit you cheerfully pull out of a drawer at 6:30 a.m. Start at whichever tier matches your real budget, brew with it for a couple of months, and let the upgrades come from your own dissatisfaction rather than from a forum thread or a YouTube ad. The coffee you make in your kitchen will be better than anything you can buy at most cafés inside a year. That is the part nobody tells you when you start, and it is the part that keeps people in this hobby for life.
