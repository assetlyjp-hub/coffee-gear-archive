I've owned both an AeroPress and a French press for years, and they live about a foot apart on my kitchen shelf. People assume they compete for the same job — they're both immersion brewers, after all — but in practice I reach for them on completely different mornings. This guide is the comparison I wish someone had handed me before I bought either one.

If you only have room (or budget) for one, I'll help you pick. And if you're the type who already suspects the answer is "both," I'll explain why that's a perfectly reasonable conclusion too.

## How Each Brewer Actually Works

Both methods steep coffee directly in water — that's what makes them immersion brewers — but the way they finish the brew is where they split.

### AeroPress: Immersion Plus Pressure

The AeroPress is a plastic cylinder with a plunger. You drop a small paper filter into the cap, add ground coffee, pour in hot water, stir, and let it steep for anywhere from 30 seconds to two minutes. Then you press the plunger down by hand, forcing the water through a paper filter and into your cup below.

That press is the magic. You're not just letting gravity do the work — you're adding gentle air pressure, which speeds extraction and pushes the brew through paper. The paper filter traps oils and fine particles, so the cup lands somewhere between a French press and a pour over.

### French Press: Steep and Plunge

The French press (also called a press pot or cafetière) is the simplest full-immersion brewer there is. You add coarse grounds, pour hot water over them, wait about four minutes, then press a metal mesh plunger down to separate the grounds from the liquid. There's no pressure and no paper — just a metal screen holding the coffee back while you pour.

Because the filter is metal mesh, oils and some fine sediment pass straight into your cup. That's the defining difference, and it shapes everything about how the two coffees taste.

## Flavor Profile: Clean vs Heavy

This is the comparison that matters most, so let me be honest about it.

**AeroPress tastes clean.** The paper filter strips out most of the oils and nearly all the sediment, so you get a bright, smooth cup with clear flavor separation. If your beans have fruity or floral notes, the AeroPress lets them sing. It's closer to a pour over than most people expect — sometimes I describe it as "pour over with training wheels." For a deeper look at the technique, my [AeroPress complete guide](/magazine/aeropress-complete-guide) walks through recipes and the inverted method.

**French press tastes heavy and oily.** Those natural coffee oils that the AeroPress filters out? The French press keeps every drop. The result is a full-bodied, round, almost syrupy cup with a velvety mouthfeel. Dark and medium roasts shine here — chocolate, caramel, and nutty depth come through richly. The trade-off is a little muddiness and a layer of fine sediment that settles at the bottom of your mug.

Neither is "better." They're different cups for different moods. If you've already read my comparison of [French press vs pour over](/magazine/french-press-vs-pour-over), think of the AeroPress as sitting right in the middle of those two extremes.

## Body and Sediment

Body is the weight and texture of the coffee in your mouth, and here the French press wins on sheer richness.

- **French press:** Heavy body, noticeable oils, and fine sediment at the bottom of the cup. Some people love the texture; others are bothered by the grit on the last sip. A coarser grind and a careful pour reduce it, but you'll never eliminate it entirely with a metal filter.
- **AeroPress:** Medium body, clean texture, essentially zero sediment thanks to the paper filter. If you want even more clarity, you can stack two paper filters or use a metal AeroPress filter for a heavier cup (a clever way to mimic French press body without the mess).

A quick note on grit: most French press sediment comes from too many fines in your grind. A burr grinder with consistent particle size makes a huge difference. ## Cleanup: The AeroPress Lands a Knockout

I'll be blunt — cleanup is the single biggest reason I reach for the AeroPress on busy mornings.

**AeroPress cleanup takes about ten seconds.** You unscrew the cap, hold it over the trash or compost, and push the plunger the rest of the way. The spent puck of coffee and the paper filter pop out in one tidy disc. A quick rinse of the rubber seal and you're done. No grounds in the sink, no scrubbing.

**French press cleanup is a chore.** You've got a basket full of wet, heavy grounds clinging to the bottom of a glass carafe. You can't pour them down the drain (they'll clog it), so you scoop or rinse them into the trash, then disassemble the three-part plunger to clean coffee oils and fines out of the mesh. Do this every day and it gets old fast.

If you hate dishes, the AeroPress wins this category outright.

## Batch Size: French Press for Groups

Here's where the French press gets its revenge.

The AeroPress makes **one cup at a time** — roughly 8 to 10 ounces, or a concentrated shot you dilute up to a mug. There are larger workarounds, but fundamentally it's a single-serve brewer.

A French press scales easily. An 8-cup (1-liter) Chambord brews enough for two or three people in one go, and 12-cup models exist for bigger crowds. If you regularly make coffee for a partner, family, or guests, the French press is simply more practical. Brewing four AeroPress cups in a row is a real time sink.

So the rule of thumb: **AeroPress for solo, French press for company.**

## Travel and Portability

If you take coffee on the road, the AeroPress is in a league of its own.

The standard AeroPress is light, made of nearly indestructible plastic, and nests its parts together. The [AeroPress Go](/magazine/aeropress-complete-guide) takes it further — it packs into its own travel mug with a lid, making it the best brewer I know for camping, hotel rooms, and office desks. I've made genuinely good coffee in a tent with one.

A French press, by contrast, is usually glass and bulky. Travel versions with insulated stainless steel exist (and they're decent), but you're still carrying a larger, heavier object with a carafe that can crack or retain odors. For backpacking or air travel, it's not the obvious choice.

**Travel verdict: AeroPress, no contest.**

## Durability

- **AeroPress:** The body is BPA-free plastic that shrugs off drops. The only wear part is the rubber plunger seal, which loosens after a few years — and AeroPress sells cheap replacements. Mine has survived being knocked off the counter more times than I'd like to admit.
- **French press:** The classic Bodum Chambord uses a borosilicate glass carafe. It's beautiful, but glass breaks. Stainless steel models (like those from Espro or Frieling) are far more durable and keep coffee hotter, though they cost more and you can't watch the brew.

If clumsiness is a concern, go AeroPress or a steel French press.

## Cost

Both are affordable, which is part of their charm.

- **AeroPress Original:** Around $35–40, including a lifetime supply of starter filters. Replacement filters are a few dollars per 350.
- **AeroPress Go:** Around $40, with the travel mug included.
- **French press:** A Bodum Chambord runs about $30–45 depending on size. No ongoing filter cost — the metal mesh is permanent, which is a small environmental and budget win over time.

Neither will break the bank. The bigger investment for either is a good grinder, which matters far more to your cup quality than the brewer itself.

## Grind Size for Each

This trips up beginners, so let's be precise.

- **French press wants coarse grounds** — think coarse sea salt or breadcrumbs. Too fine and you'll get a bitter, over-extracted, gritty mess that's hard to plunge. Coarse grounds also reduce sediment.
- **AeroPress is flexible** — medium-fine to fine works depending on your recipe. A finer grind with a short steep gives you a concentrated, espresso-style shot; a medium grind with a longer steep gives you a smooth, full cup.

This flexibility is one of the AeroPress's quiet strengths — you can dial it toward bright and tea-like or rich and intense just by changing grind and time.

## Simple Recipes to Start

### My Everyday French Press

1. 30g coarse coffee to 500g water (a 1:16 ratio).
2. Pour water just off the boil (about 200°F / 93°C) over the grounds.
3. Stir gently, place the lid on with the plunger up, and wait 4 minutes.
4. Press slowly and pour immediately so it doesn't keep extracting.

### My Everyday AeroPress

1. 15g medium-fine coffee, filter rinsed, to about 220g water (around 200°F).
2. Pour, stir for 10 seconds, and steep for 1 minute.
3. Press gently over 20–30 seconds. Stop when you hear the hiss.
4. Dilute with hot water to taste if it's too strong.

Both are starting points — tweak the ratio and time until the cup matches your taste.

## Honest Downsides

Let me not oversell either.

**The AeroPress's weaknesses:** It's single-serve, full stop — making coffee for a group is tedious. The plastic-and-rubber aesthetic isn't elegant on a nice counter. And it requires a tiny bit of technique (grind, time, press speed) to get consistent results, though it's still very forgiving.

**The French press's weaknesses:** Cleanup is genuinely annoying day after day. The sediment and oils aren't for everyone. Glass models break. And it's unforgiving of a bad grinder — fines turn your cup muddy and bitter fast. It also drops in temperature during the long steep, so it's not ideal in a cold kitchen.

## Which Should You Buy?

After all this, here's my plain advice:

- **Buy the AeroPress if** you mostly brew for yourself, value a clean cup, hate doing dishes, or travel. It's the more versatile single-serve tool.
- **Buy the French press if** you often brew for two or more, love a rich and heavy body, and don't mind a little cleanup. It's the better group brewer.
- **Buy both if** you can — they genuinely don't overlap. I use the AeroPress on solo weekday mornings and the French press on lazy weekends with company. Together they cost less than one entry-level espresso machine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Which makes stronger coffee, AeroPress or French press?**
The AeroPress can make more concentrated coffee because you control the ratio tightly and can brew an espresso-style shot. The French press makes a fuller-bodied but typically less concentrated cup. "Strong" depends on whether you mean intensity (AeroPress can win) or body (French press wins).

**2. Is AeroPress coffee like espresso?**
Not really. It can make a concentrated, intense shot, but it lacks the 9 bars of pressure real espresso needs for crema and that signature texture. Think of it as a strong, smooth concentrate rather than true espresso.

**3. Does the French press really have that much sediment?**
Some, yes. With a coarse, uniform grind and a careful pour, it's a thin layer at the very bottom you simply don't drink. A bad grinder makes it far worse. The AeroPress's paper filter eliminates sediment almost entirely.

**4. Can I make multiple cups with an AeroPress?**
Only one at a time, realistically. You can brew a strong concentrate and dilute it to fill a larger mug, but for serving several people, the French press is the practical choice.

**5. Which is easier for a complete beginner?**
The French press is the most forgiving method to start — steep, press, pour. The AeroPress is also beginner-friendly but rewards a little experimentation. Both are far easier than pour over or espresso.

**6. Do I need a special grinder for either?**
You need a burr grinder for consistent results with both, but it matters more for the French press, where fines cause grit and bitterness. The AeroPress tolerates an inconsistent grind better thanks to its paper filter.

**7. Are paper filters a dealbreaker for the AeroPress?**
For some. They're cheap and produce a cleaner cup, but they're a recurring (tiny) cost and a bit of waste. If you prefer zero ongoing filters, a reusable metal AeroPress filter exists — or the French press has no paper at all.

**8. Which is better for travel?**
The AeroPress, easily. It's light, durable plastic, and the Go model packs into its own mug. A glass French press is the last thing you want in a backpack.

## The Bottom Line

The AeroPress and French press aren't rivals so much as two specialists. The AeroPress is the clean, fast, travel-ready single-serve brewer; the French press is the rich, full-bodied group brewer. Pick based on how you actually drink coffee — solo or social, clean or heavy, low-effort or hands-on. And if your shelf has room for both, you'll never regret it.
